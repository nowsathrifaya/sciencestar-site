💡 Part 1 — Simple explanation

How Flowering Plants Reproduce

Flowering plants go through a continuous cycle: seed → seedling → adult plant → flowers → pollination → fertilisation → fruit and seeds → dispersal → and back to seed again. This loop repeats across generations, and each part of the cycle has a specific purpose that helps the plant survive and spread.

Two of the most important and frequently tested parts of this cycle are germination (when a seed starts to grow) and seed dispersal (how seeds travel away from the parent plant to grow in new locations).

🇸🇬 Part 2 — Real-life Singapore examples

Singapore Plants and Their Dispersal Methods

Singapore's tropical climate and diverse habitats mean you can find examples of all five dispersal methods without leaving the island.

The angsana trees lining many Singapore roads and expressways disperse seeds by wind — each seed has papery wings that spin like helicopter blades, slowing the descent and allowing the wind to carry them horizontally. You can find their winged seeds on the ground under angsana trees after they fruit.

Coconut palms at East Coast Park are the classic water dispersal example. The fibrous, waterproof outer husk traps air, making the coconut buoyant. Coconuts can float for months and have colonised beaches across the entire Indo-Pacific region this way.

Rambutan, mango, and durian trees in community gardens and kampong-style parks rely on animals. The sweet, fleshy fruit attracts animals (including humans) who eat the fruit and deposit the seeds elsewhere in their droppings or by discarding the stone.

The balsam plant (commonly found in Singapore gardens and roadsides) is an example of explosive dispersal — when the elongated seed pod dries, tension builds up until it suddenly bursts, flinging seeds outward. You can trigger this by gently touching a ripe pod.

🔬 Part 3 — Germination: what does a seed really need?

Water, Warmth, and Air — NOT Sunlight

One of the most important facts to get right is what a seed needs to germinate. Many students assume sunlight is required because plants need it to survive — but this is wrong for germination.

  • Water: Softens the seed coat, allowing the embryo to break through. Activates enzymes inside the seed that begin breaking down stored food. Without water, the seed stays dormant indefinitely.
  • Warmth: Chemical reactions inside the seed (enzyme activity) only work efficiently at suitable temperatures. Too cold and the reactions stop; too hot and the enzymes are destroyed.
  • Air (oxygen): The germinating seed uses its stored food reserves through respiration to get the energy it needs to grow. Respiration requires oxygen. A waterlogged seed with no air will not germinate.
  • NOT sunlight: Seeds germinate underground or in dark conditions. They use food stored in the cotyledon (seed leaf), not photosynthesis. Sunlight is only needed once the seedling's leaves emerge above ground and it can begin making its own food.
💡
To test this in an exam scenario: if a student places seeds in a dark box with moist soil at room temperature, they will germinate — proving sunlight is not needed for germination.

🔍 Part 5 — The "Why"

Why Must Seeds Be Dispersed Away from the Parent Plant?

If all seeds fell directly under the parent plant, several serious problems would arise. First, the seedlings would be growing in the shade of the parent, getting far less sunlight for photosynthesis. Second, the parent plant's roots would already be absorbing most of the available water and minerals from the surrounding soil. Third, having many plants of the same species crowded together makes them all more vulnerable to disease — one pathogen can wipe out the whole group.

Dispersal solves all three problems by spreading seeds to new locations where they have space, light, water, and minerals of their own. Each dispersal method is a brilliant adaptation that matches the seed's physical structure to the most reliable dispersal agent available in its environment.

This also explains why fruits evolved in the first place: the sweet, nutritious flesh is a deliberate "reward" to attract animals, which then carry the seeds far away before depositing them.

🚨 Part 6 — Exam traps

Common Mistakes

❌
Trap 1 — Seeds need sunlight to germinate
WRONG. Seeds need water, warmth, and air. Sunlight is NOT needed for germination. The seed uses stored food, not photosynthesis, to power its early growth.
❌
Trap 2 — All fruits are fleshy and sweet
Botanically, a fruit is any structure that develops from the ovary of a flower after fertilisation. The winged angsana "seed" is actually a fruit (the ovary wall forms the wing). Coconuts are fruits. Many dry pods are fruits. Not all fruits are edible.
❌
Trap 3 — Pollination = fertilisation
Pollination is just pollen landing on the stigma. Fertilisation is the actual joining of the pollen nucleus with the ovule nucleus. They are separate events — pollination must happen before fertilisation can occur.
📌 Part 7 — Mini summary

Key Points at a Glance

  • Germination needs: water + warmth + air (oxygen). NOT sunlight
  • Seeds use stored food in cotyledon for germination, not photosynthesis
  • 5 dispersal methods: wind, water, animal-eaten, animal-hooks, explosive
  • Dispersal reduces competition with parent plant for light, water, and minerals
  • After fertilisation: ovule → seed, ovary wall → fruit
  • Pollination ≠ fertilisation — pollination comes first

💦 Xylem vs Phloem — Transport Systems

XYLEM
Carries: Water + dissolved minerals
Direction: UPWARD only (roots → stem → leaves)
How: pulled up by transpiration from leaves
Memory tip: X(ylem) = water going up like an X-press lift
PHLOEM
Carries: Food (glucose/sugar) made in leaves
Direction: Both UP and DOWN
How: food distributed from leaves to all plant parts
Memory tip: P(hloem) = food flows both ways (Ph = food)
📝 Plants — PSLE Exam Tips
XYLEM vs PHLOEM
Xylem = water upward only. Phloem = food both ways. Never mix these up — it is one of the most common plant errors.
ROOT HAIR CELLS
Root hair cells have a LARGE surface area to absorb more water and minerals efficiently from the soil.
WILTING
Plant wilts when transpiration rate exceeds absorption rate. More water is lost through leaves than gained through roots.
STOMATA LOCATION
Stomata (pores) are mainly on the LOWER surface of the leaf. This reduces water loss from direct sunlight hitting the pores.

Exam technique — Plant Life Cycles

The single most tested distinction in plant reproduction is pollination versus fertilisation — and the most common way students lose marks is conflating the two. Pollination is a physical event that happens outside the plant: pollen is transferred from anther to stigma. Fertilisation is a chemical event that happens inside the ovary: the male sex cell from the pollen tube fuses with the female egg cell inside the ovule. You cannot have fertilisation without pollination first. In PSLE answers, always define both, state that they are different events, and state the correct sequence.

For seed dispersal questions, the structure of a full-marks answer is always: feature → function → why that function aids dispersal. "The Angsana seed has a flat papery wing. As the seed falls, the wing causes it to spin like a helicopter rotor, slowing its descent. The slower fall gives wind more time to carry the seed further from the parent tree." Three sentences. Three marks. Every feature-function-dispersal question follows this pattern — learn the structure and apply it to any seed, even one you have never seen before.

  • Pollination first, fertilisation second. Always in this order. Pollination does not guarantee fertilisation — different species pollen will not fertilise.
  • Germination requires water, warmth, and oxygen — not sunlight. Seeds germinate underground in total darkness.
  • Ovule → seed; ovary wall → fruit. After fertilisation, always in this direction.
  • Asexual reproduction = genetically identical offspring. Faster than sexual reproduction but no genetic variation.
  • Self-pollination vs cross-pollination: cross-pollination produces genetically varied offspring, which is why most plants favour it.

Questions students ask

Why do plants produce so much more pollen than they have ovules?

Wind-pollinated plants produce millions of pollen grains because wind dispersal is imprecise — the vast majority never reach a compatible stigma. Producing enormous quantities compensates for this. Insect-pollinated plants produce less pollen but invest more in making it sticky and attractive to specific pollinators that will carry it accurately. The trade-off is quantity versus precision.

Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable?

Scientifically, a tomato is a fruit — it develops from the ovary of a flower and contains seeds. In science, a fruit is any structure that develops from the ovary after fertilisation. Many "vegetables" in the kitchen (cucumber, pumpkin, chilli, avocado, peas in their pods) are scientifically fruits. "Vegetable" is a culinary term, not a scientific one.
