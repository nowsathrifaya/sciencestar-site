Light & Shadows P3/P4 Science: Notes, Diagrams, Questions & Free Quiz

Understand how light travels, transparent vs opaque materials, shadow formation, reflection, and how to ace every light-related question in the PSLE exam.

Light is one of the most important topics in Singapore Primary Science. It begins in P3 with basic concepts and builds toward more detailed understanding by PSLE. Students must understand how light behaves, what materials do to light, how shadows are formed, and how we can use light in everyday situations. This guide covers everything you need from P3 through to PSLE.

Properties of Light — P3/P4 Notes & Key PSLE Concepts

Light is a form of energy. The most important property you need to know for PSLE is that light travels in straight lines. This is called rectilinear propagation of light. It explains why shadows have sharp edges, why we cannot see around corners, and how periscopes work.

Light travels extremely fast — about 300,000 km per second in a vacuum. For Primary Science purposes, you just need to know that light travels much faster than sound, which is why we see lightning before we hear thunder.

Transparent, Translucent & Opaque Materials — P3 Notes & Questions

Different materials interact with light differently. For PSLE, you need to classify materials correctly and explain what happens to light when it meets each type.

TypeWhat Happens to LightExamples
TransparentLight passes through completely; objects can be seen clearlyClear glass, clean water, air
TranslucentSome light passes through; objects seen are blurry or unclearFrosted glass, wax paper, thin cloth, tissue paper
OpaqueNo light passes through; objects cannot be seen through itWood, metal, cardboard, brick wall

⚠ Common PSLE Mistake

Students often say translucent materials "block all light." This is incorrect — translucent materials allow some light through, but scatter it so images appear blurry. Only opaque materials block all light. Also, a shadow forms when an opaque or translucent object blocks light — not just opaque objects.

How Shadows Are Formed — P3/P4 Notes, Diagrams & PSLE Questions

A shadow forms when an opaque (or translucent) object blocks the path of light. Because light travels in straight lines, it cannot bend around the object — the area behind the object receives no light, forming a dark region called the shadow.

For a shadow to form, three things are needed: a light source, an opaque or translucent object, and a surface (screen) for the shadow to fall on.

Factors That Affect Shadow Size

  • Position of the light source — when the light source is closer to the object, the shadow is larger; when it is further away, the shadow is smaller
  • Position of the object — when the object is closer to the screen, the shadow is smaller and sharper; when further from the screen, the shadow is larger and blurrier
  • Size of the object — a larger object creates a larger shadow
  • Angle of the light source — the angle affects the shape of the shadow (e.g. the Sun's angle changes shadow length throughout the day)

Shadows and the Sun

In Singapore, the Sun appears to move across the sky from east to west during the day (due to Earth's rotation). This means shadows change direction and length throughout the day. At noon, when the Sun is highest in the sky, shadows are shortest. In the morning and late afternoon, shadows are longest. This is a favourite PSLE question type — you may be asked to identify what time of day a shadow suggests.

Reflection of Light — P4 Notes, Mirror Laws & PSLE Questions

When light hits a smooth, shiny surface it bounces back — this is called reflection. Mirrors are the best example of reflection. The angle at which light hits a mirror (angle of incidence) equals the angle at which it bounces off (angle of reflection).

Reflection is why we can see objects that do not emit their own light. The Moon, for example, has no light of its own — we see it because it reflects light from the Sun.

A periscope uses two mirrors positioned at 45° angles to allow someone to see over or around obstacles. Light enters the top, reflects off the first mirror downward, then reflects off the second mirror toward the viewer's eye.

Uses of Light in Daily Life — Singapore Examples & Exam Applications

  • Periscopes in submarines use mirrors to see above the water surface
  • Road signs and safety clothing use reflective materials to be seen in the dark
  • Solar panels convert light energy into electrical energy
  • Greenhouses use glass (transparent) to let sunlight in while trapping heat
  • Frosted glass (translucent) in bathroom windows provides privacy while letting light through

PSLE Light & Shadows Exam Questions — Worked Model Answers

Question: Ahmad places his hand between a torch and a wall. Explain why a shadow forms on the wall.

Model Answer: Light from the torch travels in straight lines. When the light reaches Ahmad's hand, the hand (being opaque) blocks the light from passing through. Since light cannot bend around the hand, the area behind the hand on the wall receives no light. This dark region is the shadow.

Question: The shadow of a pole is long in the morning but short at noon. Explain why.

Model Answer: In the morning, the Sun is low in the sky. Light hits the pole at a low angle, casting a long shadow. At noon, the Sun is almost directly overhead. Light hits the pole from nearly above, so the shadow is very short. The angle of the light source determines the length of the shadow.

📋 Key Facts Summary

  • Light travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation)
  • Transparent: light passes through fully (clear glass, water)
  • Translucent: some light passes through, image blurry (frosted glass, tissue)
  • Opaque: no light passes through (wood, metal, brick)
  • Shadow forms when an opaque/translucent object blocks light; needs light source + object + screen
  • Shadow is larger when light source is closer to the object
  • Shadow is smaller when object is closer to the screen
  • Shadows are shortest at noon (Sun directly overhead) and longest in morning/evening
  • Reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection; periscopes use two 45° mirrors

🧠 Key Points to Remember
  • Light travels in straight lines — this explains shadow formation
  • Transparent: all light passes through (clear glass); Translucent: some light passes through (frosted glass); Opaque: no light passes through (wood) — shadow forms
  • Shadow size increases when object moves closer to light source
  • Shadow size decreases when object moves further from light source (or closer to screen)
  • Reflection: light bounces off a surface; smooth surfaces reflect more evenly than rough ones
  • We see objects because light reflects off them into our eyes
  • Light travels much faster than sound — we see lightning before we hear thunder

Practice Questions

📝 Practice Question 1
A student shines a torch at three different materials: clear glass, frosted glass, and a wooden board. Describe what happens to the light in each case.
(3 marks)
▼ Show Answer
✅ Clear glass (transparent): Light passes through almost completely — objects can be seen clearly through it. Frosted glass (translucent): Light passes through partially but is scattered — objects cannot be seen clearly, only as blurry outlines. Wooden board (opaque): No light passes through — a shadow is formed on the other side.
📝 Practice Question 2
A child stands 1 metre from a light source and casts a shadow on a wall. The child moves closer to the light source. Describe and explain what happens to the shadow.
(2 marks)
▼ Show Answer
✅ The shadow becomes larger. When the child moves closer to the light source, the child blocks a larger angle of light from the source, causing a bigger shadow to be cast on the wall.
📝 Practice Question 3
Explain why you cannot see around a corner without a mirror.
(2 marks)
▼ Show Answer
✅ Light travels in straight lines — it cannot bend around corners. A mirror can be used to reflect light around a corner, changing the direction of the light rays so they reach your eyes.

Light & Shadows PSLE Exam Technique — How to Score Full Marks

Shadow questions catch students out because they look deceptively simple. The key is knowing all three requirements for a shadow to form: a light source, an opaque object that blocks the light, and a surface for the shadow to fall on. Miss any one of these in your explanation and the answer is incomplete. A common error is writing "the shadow formed because the object blocked the light" without naming the light source or the surface — one-sentence answers that feel complete but are missing elements the mark scheme checks.

The transparent-translucent-opaque distinction is tested more precisely than most students expect. Transparent does not mean "clear" in the everyday sense. A red glass bottle is transparent — light passes through clearly — even though you cannot see objects clearly through coloured glass. The scientific definition is about whether light passes through without significant scattering. Translucent means light passes through but is scattered, so objects cannot be seen clearly. Opaque means no light passes through at all. Know the definition and give a concrete Singapore example for each: glass window (transparent), frosted glass shower screen (translucent), concrete wall (opaque).

  • Three requirements for a shadow: light source + opaque object + surface. Name all three in shadow formation answers.
  • Luminous = produces its own light. The Moon is non-luminous — it only reflects sunlight. Stars are luminous. Mirrors are non-luminous.
  • Shadow size: object closer to light source = bigger shadow; object closer to screen = smaller, sharper shadow. Know both relationships.
  • Angle of incidence = angle of reflection. This is why periscopes work — two mirrors at 45° redirect light through a corner.
  • Light travels in straight lines always. This is the reason shadows exist, the reason you cannot see around corners, and the reason periscopes need two mirrors.

Questions students ask

Why does a shadow have sharp edges in sunlight but blurry edges on a cloudy day?

In sunlight, the Sun acts as a small, distant point source — all light rays travel nearly parallel, creating a sharp, well-defined shadow. On a cloudy day the whole sky acts as a large diffuse source, sending light from many angles simultaneously. Each angle creates a slightly different shadow position, and these overlapping shadows produce a soft, blurry edge. This is also why cloudy days produce more flattering photographs — the soft, diffuse light eliminates harsh shadow lines under eyes and chins.

Why does your shadow change length through the day?

The Sun appears to move across the sky from the perspective of a person standing on Earth, changing the angle at which light hits the ground. When the Sun is low (early morning, late afternoon), light strikes at a shallow angle and shadows are very long. When the Sun is high at midday, light comes almost straight down and shadows are short. In Singapore, very close to the equator, the Sun is almost directly overhead at certain times of year and shadows nearly disappear completely — something that only happens in a narrow band of countries near the equator.
